Grassroots Resistance with Amplify

Context

After leaving Quora, I looked for ways to use technology to empower the new progressive resistance to President Trump. I met the founders of Amplify, an app used by grassroots groups to coordinate taking actions. Early versions of the product had met with success: thousands of people each month were coordinating calling congress, organizing community meetings, and sending postcards to swing voters. I worked with the founders to increase the stickyness and power of the platform.

Role

On this project I worked directly with the app's cofounders on all parts of the product development process: brainstorming feature ideas, building wireframes and high fidelity mockups, then implementing all designs in the React Native app.

Work

The product had a core group of dedicated users, so we started by spending time with them and learning just why the experience was so good for them. These interviews led to two key hypotheses for why Amplify improved the work of political resistence. We focused our product development on improving what was already great about the product, with a focus on solving two key problems:

Political action is lonely. Many impactful political actions (like calling a congressperson to protest a bill) happen at home, by yourself, and no one ever finds out about it. We heard from our users that that positive social reinforcement made these actions more satisfying and thus more frequently taken. This seemed especially promising, because our users were all members of an offline local grassroots group (e.g. a local Indivisible chapter) so had existing social relationships, which made feedback shared through the app especially compelling. The first version of the app was centered around the actions to be taken, with a secondary screen showing who had taken each action. Our first major project was reorganizing the app to make the feed of activity more prominent, and adding the necessary pieces to make the app feel social: profile photos to lend humanity to each person, large Like buttons to encourage the positive reinforcement, and a Commenting feature to facilitate encouragement and momentum:

We learned from our users that the most compelling experience was knowing they inspired others to act. So we started tracking when a person's post inspired someone else to take action, and started sending push notifications when this happened:

With this foundation of interpersonal interactions, we moved to our second key problem:

There's not enough to do. Much of the app centered around calling elected officials, which could feel futile — a call wouldn’t lead to a clear outcome (e.g. a changed piece of legislation), and while Democrats were in the minority, it wasn’t clear what effect all these calls were having. User interviews revealed that people were hungry for more ways to take action. We brainstormed ways that individuals could take impactful action from their phones.

As the 2018 midterms neared, we knew that voter registration and turnout would be critical, and that social pressure was an effective tool for increasing the midterm turnout. We built a feature that took a person’s address book, compared it to national voter records, and encouraged texts to be sent to friends who weren’t yet registered. Our hypothesis was that this action would be seen as more directly impactful, and would power the flywheel of Action → Social Reinforcement → More Action.

We also produced a demo video of this feature for our users to understand the feature.

Conclusion

I wrote a long essay on my lessons from working at Amplify. In short, I believe that the best experiences center around an action that people love to do repeatedly and frequently. Politics does not offer many of these actions (those that exist are slow, cyclical, and rarely offer clear results). The few actions that are impactful are socially delicate, and are very difficult to accelerate using technology. Amplify was an incredible opportunity to rapidly test hypotheses on a small and engaged community of activists, and ultimately helped demonstrate to me just how hard it is to build compelling digital experiences in this space.
